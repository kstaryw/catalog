The catalog data from 1996 to 2024 reveals a significant transformation in educational offerings, marked by a growth factor of 4.62 times the number of subjects available. This expansion reflects broader trends in higher education, where institutions are increasingly diversifying their programs to meet the evolving demands of the workforce and society. The shift from a focus on traditional topics such as "theory" and "analysis" to contemporary themes like "engineering" and "science" indicates a response to technological advancements and the need for specialized skills.

The most notable growth occurred in Department 6, which saw a staggering increase of 402.99% from 67 to 337 subjects. This department's expansion may correlate with the rising importance of interdisciplinary studies and the integration of technology into various fields. Similarly, Department 15's growth of 212% suggests a robust response to emerging industries, possibly reflecting the increasing relevance of data science and analytics in today's job market.

The introduction of Department 21G, which had no subjects in 1996 but now offers 214, underscores the emergence of entirely new fields of study. This trend is indicative of the rapid pace of innovation and the necessity for educational institutions to adapt quickly to new knowledge areas. The establishment of new departments is a strategic move to attract students interested in cutting-edge disciplines.

Conversely, the discontinuation of subjects in Department 13, which saw a complete reduction to zero offerings, highlights the challenges faced by traditional disciplines that may no longer align with current educational or industry needs. This shift emphasizes the importance of adaptability in curriculum design, as institutions must continuously evaluate the relevance of their programs.

The metrics also reveal a slight decrease in breadth and average subject offerings per department, suggesting a potential consolidation of programs. While the entropy measure indicates a relatively stable diversity in subjects, the overall trend points towards a more focused curriculum that prioritizes depth in specific areas rather than a broad array of topics.

The introduction of 25 new subjects alongside the discontinuation of just one reflects a proactive approach to curriculum development. This balance suggests that educational institutions are not only responding to market demands but are also anticipating future trends, preparing students for careers that may not yet exist.

In summary, the catalog changes from 1996 to 2024 illustrate a dynamic educational landscape that is increasingly responsive to the needs of students and the labor market. The growth in specialized fields, the emergence of new departments, and the strategic discontinuation of less relevant subjects all point to a forward-thinking approach in higher education, aligning with broader societal shifts towards innovation and specialization.
